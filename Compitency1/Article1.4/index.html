Give Advice

Giving advice is a great way to support your colleagues at work. Draw on your company experience and job duties to advise other team members.

Use these tips to help guide your advice:

  • Ask: Before offering advice, be sure it is something your colleague/customer wants.
  • Be kind: Try not to judge your colleague/customer and give advice that is kind and helpful.
  • Be honest: Honesty helps ensure that the advice you give is helpful.
  • Listen: Listen to what your colleague/customer says and how they respond to your advice.
  • Collaborate: When giving advice, try to find a solution together to fix the colleague's/customers’ problem.

Communicating With Colleagues

Helps build a relationship and can inspire teamwork.

Here are ways to establish good communication:

  • Greet coworkers/customers: Say hello to your colleagues/customers when you see them. Please make an effort to acknowledge them.
  • Check in: Ask how your colleagues/customers feel and how their day goes.
  • Listen: Pay attention to what they say.
  • Consider nonverbal cues such as eye contact and positive body language: Maintaining eye contact is essential for respectful communication as well as showing an open and upright posture to show you are engaged and confident.
  • Show respect: Be respectful of boundaries and be conscious of how you behave when communicating.

Consider establishing a channel or method by which communication is easy and comfortable for all team members, such as a social media group or a work app for an employee chat, or you could schedule weekly meetings.

Clarify & Respond Appropriately

Ask relevant questions to make sure you understand. Make them open-ended questions, if possible. A “yes” or “no” may confirm, but an explanation offers more information—probe for feelings.

Sometimes the emotions behind the words are more important than the words themselves.

Be open and honest in your responses. Share your thoughts, insights, and feelings, respectfully, and respectfully.

You can acknowledge the speaker’s concerns and ideas even if you disagree…especially when you disagree.

Keep an Open-Mind

Defer judgments, whether agreement or disagreement, and don’t make assumptions. Wait until the speaker is finished before formulating opinions.

It’s hard not to think about what you’ll say next, especially if there’s disagreement, but you miss what is being said if you’re thinking about your response.

Challenge Traditional Ways of Thinking and Doing

Ask questions when appropriate if you hear something that needs to be corrected.

Always search for ways to improve an existing process or re-imagine problem solutions.

Use your curiosity and critical thinking skills to evaluate the best decision for the consumer.

Get Some Rest

When you're tired, you're more likely to choose the easiest or most comfortable decision. This is known as decision fatigue.

Make sure you get a good night's sleep and are clear-headed when possible for big decisions.

If that's not possible, step back for 30 minutes and clear your mind before coming back to the decision.

Make a Plan, Work Through a List and Weigh The Options

If you know you have an upcoming decision to make, it can help to make a plan.

  • Prioritizing your decisions might help you improve your decision-making and time-management skills.

Make a list of items you need to accomplish or decide on, starting with the smallest decision. Working your way through the list can make you feel empowered and achieved, improving your confidence in decisions.

  • Consider making a list of all the positives and negatives for each choice. This can help you examine all your preferences and options in an informed manner. It could help you think of additional items to consider.

Laying it out in a list can help you look at your decision in a structured format and objectively at all the advantages and disadvantages.

This can help you improve your analytical, critical thinking, and problem-solving skills.

Learn from the Past and Practice

Training your brain by practicing decisions can help you decide quickly in the actual moment.

  • First, you might try practicing making simple decisions, such as whether you will use color or black ink to print a report.
  • Then, once you feel confident with more minor decisions, try practicing more significant ones, such as which company to buy a copy machine from.
  • Take lessons from the past and use them to improve your decision-making abilities. Previous decisions you've made can be helpful when considering a new decision.
  • Try analyzing an earlier decision and its outcome, and use that information to guide a recent, related decision.

Limit Choices

The more choices you have, the more complex your decision-making process.

  • Limiting your choices can help you consider just a few options at a time so you make a more informed decision.
  • Restricting your choices also helps you learn to think critically when considering the remaining options.

For example, when choosing a photo for your company's social media account, focus on images that tell your company's story most simply and get rid of ones that make a visitor have to think about it.
