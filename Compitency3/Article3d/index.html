Collaboration

Helping others in a day-to-day approach, working for the common good and keeping a positive work atmosphere. Ensuring everyone gets what is needed to succeed

Positive Behaviours
  • Fosters a good working atmosphere.
  • Helps colleagues when noticing they are in need.
  • Shares less enjoyable duties to ensure there is good balance in the team tasks.
  • Shares success with others in the team, and works for a common goal.
  • Provides constructive feedback to allow colleagues’ professional development.

Development Tips for Improving – COLLABORATION

As per the individual skills gap report, try the following:

  • To foster collaboration, try to jump in more often when a colleague or customer asks for help and support, volunteer for a team task and focus on team objective and on how each person brings value to it.
  • Think of how you can make compromises to integrate others' ideas, in case of diverging opinions on a project or a conversational topic.

Be an Active Listener

Being an active listener is just hearing what the other person is saying. An active listener listens without judgment and clarifies the meaning behind what was said, so they fully understand the point made.

A helpful active listening strategy is repeating or summarizing what you just heard from the other person to ensure you’re both on the same page.

Here's an example:

‘’To confirm, we're going to split the responsibilities of this presentation. First, you will gather the facts and figures, and I will start the slide outlines.’’

Active listening also means being in tune with nonverbal communication. Notice how people communicate and what their body language, facial expressions, and tone of voice are saying.

For example, if someone is fidgeting or having difficulty keeping eye contact, they may be nervous or uncomfortable with the situation.

Please consider that when listening to what they say and ensure you have an appropriate response based on their verbal and nonverbal communication.

Refine Your Communication Skills

Communication is essential for collaboration to succeed. The biggest tip to being a good communicator in workplace collaboration is never to assume people already know things.

  • Provide regular updates to your manager and coworkers, and be clear and succinct.
  • Keep communication open among your colleagues and never avoid sharing information needed to carry out tasks.

It’s also important to remember that people all have different communication styles.

To promote a culture of effective collaboration and communication, be mindful of these different styles and adapt your contact accordingly.

For example, some people aren’t comfortable speaking up in front of large groups, especially spontaneously.

If you need to gather ideas from your group members, try asking everyone to write two to three ideas down in advance in a shared online document and then discuss each picture in the meeting.

This might be less nerve-wracking and more approachable for more introverted coworkers, and it still allows them to contribute ideas.

Develop Your Emotional Intelligence

Emotional intelligence is the ability to identify and manage your emotions, recognize emotions in others and react appropriately, and apply your feelings to tasks.

While that sounds somewhat straightforward in theory, emotional intelligence is tough to develop and is considered by many companies to be an essential leadership skill.

Because of this, many people tap into personal coaching to help build their emotional intelligence.

Good emotional intelligence can help you collaborate better with others by responding more productively, understanding your colleagues’ perspectives, and building on your relationships with your coworkers.

Research has shown that emotionally intelligent teams are more effective, collaborate, and communicate better.

In addition, emotional intelligence usually goes hand in hand with empathy, which can help you relate to and recognize the responsibilities of your coworkers.

Seek Out Different Perspectives

A crucial part of being a successful collaborator is the ability to work with various people (not just the ones you agree with) and the willingness to seek new perspectives on issues.

It’s important to acknowledge that other people have skills, experience, and insight that you may not have but could benefit your project or team.

A good team player seeks out other employees who could bring something different to the table so they can see all sides of an issue to find the best solution.

Not only will your work be better, but you’ll also find that your company culture and employee engagement will improve by promoting an inclusive attitude and teamwork mentality in the workplace, as seen in a Deloitte study.

They found that people who collaborate at work and have access to digital collaboration tools are up to 17% more satisfied with their job and workplace culture.

This shows that collaboration and tools to collaborate effectively are critical to employee fulfilment, especially for remote employees who rely on digital tools to work with others.

Recognize Others

Usually, one person does not carry out a project’s responsibilities. Instead, it’s a team effort, with many ideas, contributions, and accomplishments from each team member.

Recognize the part others play in the team’s success when collaborating. Plus, don’t keep that observation to yourself.

Most people love to be acknowledged for their efforts, especially in front of others. So don’t be afraid to praise your coworkers for a job well done, either privately or in front of the whole team.

Recognition is also crucial to employee engagement and can help foster a more positive environment. If you work with others and give credit where credit is due, you’ll be a collaborator who is more enjoyable to work with and will help build a more robust culture of workplace collaboration.

Be Accountable and Do Your Part

A good partner on a project, or in any circumstance, isn’t afraid to work hard to get things done. Step up and offer to help if you see coworkers struggling or looking for guidance.

When you work within a group, you might need to put the needs of the team before your own, such as helping a colleague with their portion of the project.

Be accountable for the work you have a part in, meet deadlines, and put out quality work that isn’t sloppy.

Don’t let the socialization aspect of teamwork cause your job duties to suffer. Be a dependable partner to complete your responsibilities on time and with pride.

By doing your part on the team, others will appreciate your reliability and enjoy collaborating with your team.
