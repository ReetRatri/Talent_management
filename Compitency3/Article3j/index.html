Block Out Time for Thinking

Don't let a crowded schedule prevent you from thinking strategically.Â 

When things get hectic, it's easy to focus on small details instead of the bigger picture.

However, being strategic means being able to take a step back from those little details.

Clear out sometime in your schedule when you can sit and think without any distractions

Look for Trends Around You

Try to identify problems that keep reoccurring. As you look at the big picture, like your overall business operations or the scope of your personal life, look for issues you must repeatedly address.

Once you can identify those, creating a strategic plan for dealing with those obstacles will be much easier.

  • For instance, you might notice that your company has issues with shipping when there's bad weather in a specific part of the country. So you might implement a backup plan, like designating alternate shipping routes or using a secondary shipping company.
  • If you're playing a game like chess, you might notice that your opponent tends to favor a specific move when they feel threatened. Once you're aware of that, you may be able to pre-empt that moveâ€”like by blocking a particular piece, for example.
  • The best way to understand a market that you're trying to enter and the people that will use your product is to start writing down clear statements of who will be using your product and how they should feel when they use it.
  • Use your time and efforts to figure out who already is in this market space and why they won't be able to capture what you will. Then, look at three or four other players in the market that could potentially disrupt and take advantage of this opportunity in the next year or two and weigh in to see whether you need to enter the market today, wait a couple of years, or maybe not enter it at all.

Question Everything

Be curious and let go of assumptions. A big part of being strategic means challenging the status quo, so get in the habit of playing devil's advocate with yourself.

Don't be afraid to question why things are done a certain way or how they could be done differently, and dig deeper if you feel something is unclear or ambiguous.

When you do that, you'll find it much easier to come up with different approaches to solving problems.

For instance, if your office filing system gets messy, question whether there's a different way to organize the paperwork, so it's more streamlined.

If you're planning to start your own business, ask yourself questions like "How could this go wrong?" and "What kind of problems should I prepare for?"

Get Other Perspectives

Reach out to others for their opinions. Don't just surround yourself with people who agree with youâ€”build a team of people willing to tell you what they think.

Then, when you're dealing with a problem, ask those people how they'd approach the situation. When you do that, you're more likely to get a perspective you might not have considered.

This doesn't mean you need to let other people make decisions for you, but listening to different points of view can help you come up with a creative solution to whatever you're working on.

For example, if you're working as a project manager and you run into a problem, you might ask everyone from your boss to the boots-on-the-ground workers how they would solve it.

Then, you'd evaluate those perspectives to decide which suggestions merit the most.

Especially reach out to people who are regularly willing to challenge what you think, like someone who says, "Have you thought about it this way?" Those opposing viewpoints are crucial for strategic planning.

Anticipate Problems Before They Happen

Think about a variety of different potential outcomes.

Staying focused on your long-term goals is hard if you're constantly reacting. Instead, try to be proactive by anticipating changes that may be coming or issues that could arise.

When you're thinking a few steps ahead, it will be much easier to plan an effective strategy.

For instance, in a business setting, you might stay informed about any advances in technology that could cause your services to become obsoleteâ€”then figure out how to incorporate those technological advances so you can use them to your advantage.

Foreseeing every situation that could arise in your personal or professional life is impossible.

However, if you're generally prepared for different situations, it's usually easier to adapt when something unexpected does happen.

Focus on How to Make Things Better

Don't just hone in on what's wrong.

Instead, practice looking for what's going right.

When you do that, it's easier to look for what you can do to build on the good things happening, which will sometimes help eliminate problems before they begin.

If you notice a problem, be the person who looks for a solutionâ€”not just the person who points out the issue.

For example, if your team regularly comes in behind the deadline, you might strategize how to get through your workload more efficiently, so it's easier to stay on schedule.

Set Clear Goals and Evaluate Them Periodically

Be strategic by focusing on long-term targets.

Use the acronym SMART to ensure your goals are reasonableâ€”goals should be Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, and Timely. In addition, create an action plan with specific steps for how you will meet those goals.

  • For example, if your marketing strategy involves improving your social media presence, you might create an action plan that includes identifying your target audience, optimizing your pages, sticking to a regular posting schedule, and creating a consistent "voice" for your brand.
  • Regularly monitor your progress to ensure the plan is still working for you. Also, be flexible when updating your plansâ€”that's an integral part of ensuring your strategy stays relevant!
  • Keep in mind that this doesn't mean micro-managing every detail of how to meet those goals. In your personal life, you'll need to be flexible as you see how things play out. In a professional setting, giving your team some leeway as long as they're meeting their targets on time is essential.

Stay focused on Your More Important Goals

Don't let minor crises distract you.

When you're a great strategist, you understand that getting tangled up in more minor problems can derail you from your endgame.

When something comes up that you need to deal with, ask yourself what solution lines up best with your long-term goals.

That can make it easier to decide what to do without spending much time on things that aren't that important.

Give yourself time to make crucial decisionsâ€”patience is integral to strategic thinking.

For instance, if you have a goal to pay off your credit cards in a year, and your dishwasher breaks down, you might decide it's more strategic to wash your dishes by hand until you get your credit cards paid off, then save until you can afford to pay cash for a new dishwasher.

If you're running a company that aims to grow your client base, purchasing new manufacturing equipment is a good investment, mainly if slow production times affect customer satisfaction.

Work on The Weakest Part of The Plan

Focus your energy on where you need the most improvement.

Ask yourself if there's an area where you're especially vulnerable, then find ways to shore that up.

In chess, this is known as "improving your worst piece"â€”but it can apply to anything from managing your personal life to running a huge corporation.

Once you've taken care of that issue, find the new "worst piece" and work on that.

  • For example, if you want your home to be neater, you might start by identifying the spot that tends to get messy the fastest. Then, create an organizational system for that area. Once that's under control, move on to the next place where clutter tends to gather.

Balance Logic and Creativity

Use both the left and right sides of your brain.

You are strategic means that sometimes you have to come up with innovative solutions to problems.

However, you must be realistic and logical about what will and won't work.

Find a balance between the two that works for you rather than leaning too heavily on one side or the other.

For example, when developing a new product, you typically start by creating a creative solution to a problem your consumers are facing. However, you must also be logical about cost-effectiveness, customer acquisition costs, and manufacturing limitations.

Don't be afraid to take risks as you build your strategy!

Learn From Your Experiences

Reflect; don't just react.

Sometimes when we're overwhelmed, it's easy to move from one experience to another without taking time to reflect on what's happening.

However, if you want better strategic thinking skills, you must look back on your experiences to understand what happened, why it happened, and what you can do to prevent it next time.

If you spent much time developing a new product that didn't sell well, you might realize that you need to put more effort into getting feedback early in the planning process.

That way, you'll only put resources into something that generates much interest.

Learning from your mistakes is essential to becoming more strategic because it helps you avoid those stumbling blocks in the future.
