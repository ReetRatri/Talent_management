Adaptation & Flexibility

Ability to react appropriately to new situations, to multitask and integrate changes

Positive Behaviours
  • Thinks outside the box and not just to follow guidelines.
  • Stays available (as much as possible), can switch tasks or adapt schedule.
  • Stays open to change, welcome new processes or way of doing things.
  • Quickly adapts to novelty.
  • Demonstrates adaptation to others, their speech, behaviors etc...
  • Champions change in the team; supporting team members through change.

Development Tips for Improving – ADAPTION & FLEXIBILITY

As per the individual skills gap report, try the following:

  • Seek to understand what the benefits of a new approach are by experimenting it first-hand.
  • Question where this resistance to change comes from: is it about the change itself, about lack of time or strategies to integrate it?

As is the case with learning a whole range of critical soft skills, learning to navigate change better and become adaptable is best done by others.

  • For example, do you admire how Fiona from finance consistently delivers fiscal results with a positive message for the future, even if targets have been missed?
  • Or perhaps you like how Sean brings in members of different teams to brainstorm solutions to the IT team’s challenges?

Take notes when these people exhibit the adaptability skills you’re keen to learn or ask them for tips and guidance directly.

While you can learn from people inside and outside your organization, if you’re learning from those internally, you’ll get the added benefit of knowing how the skills are best applied for your organization and role.

Extracting positives from situations or projects that don’t go as planned can be challenging, mainly if the loss is a significant deadline is missed.

However, being able to find the silver lining in all the work you do is a brilliant example of adaptability skills in action, as it requires you to reset and reframe your focus, often taking a step back and viewing things less critically and more objectively.

If you find it hard to identify the positives, start by noting what you learn when things don’t go as planned, factoring this exercise in as a crucial part of your review strategy.

The more you engage in this process, the more optimism you’ll find you have, and the easier it will be to change tack and overcome challenges next time.

No one’s perfect, and making mistakes is a very human and genuine thing that will happen throughout your career. While making an error can be frustrating or even humiliating, how you manage the aftermath counts, and this phase will help improve your adaptability skills.

If you learn from your mistakes, share newfound knowledge and test alternative solutions, then chances are these mistakes will form some of the most important lessons you will learn throughout your working life.

Just as they must embrace change to grow, the best leaders and organizations among us usually haven’t achieved success by working alone.

Instead, they have inspiring mentors, innovative colleagues, and gifted networks to lean on for support, ask questions, and debate ideas.

Asking questions is a great way to learn more and challenge established ways of doing things; a key component of being adaptive is ensuring that your questions are well-researched and delivered professionally to ensure you receive the most valuable answers.
