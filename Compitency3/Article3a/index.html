Active Listening

Ability to listen, to draw meaning from what others are saying and to show it. Not only repeat what others are saying but also demonstrate a deep understanding. Making others feel they are listened to and that their story matters

Positive Behaviours
  • Is inquisitive ; asks the right questions to explore customer’s situation or needs.
  • Gives customers space to express opinions about service or products.
  • Shows deep understanding of customers’ needs.
  • Weights words and carefully choses them.
  • Takes the right time to explore customer’s needs.

Development Tips for Improving – ACTIVE LISTENING

As per the individual skills gap report, try the following:

  • To focus on meaningful conversations, some people find it helpful to prepare in advance, imagining different possible scenarios: what would be the most appropriate question to best explore a certain need?
  • Calibrating conversation to balance speaking and listening is a great way to ensure the other person in the conversation has enough space to deliver their thoughts or concerns.

Give Focused Attention

Try to minimize external distractions.

Turn down the noise, and put down or step away from what you’re doing if possible.

Also, set aside other internal thinking and dialogue. Don’t watch the clock, fidget or go over your to-do list for later.

Maintain Eye Contact

Direct eye contact shows your attention and intention to listen. This doesn’t mean staring, however.

Intense eye contact can be intimidating to some—incredibly shy or introverted.

Be reasonable, but try not to let your eyes wander to whatever is going on around you.

Smile

Facial expressions convey a lot, and a smile is open, inviting, and encouraging.

Watch Body Language

  • Be conscious of your body language.
  • Keep an open posture, a non-aggressive stance, face the speaker(s), lean in rather than away, and watch your hands, how you tilt your head, and your expressions.

(For instance, I tend to cross my arms in front of me because it feels comfortable and wrinkles my brow because I’m concentrating, but this body language can appear defensive or critical, so I need to make an effort to soften it a bit.)

  • Pay attention to the speaker’s body language as well. It works both ways.

Offer Encouragement

Nod occasionally and offer a judiciously placed ‘Yes,’ “OK.” ‘I understand”, or “Good.”

Just be careful not to overdo I or risk sounding irritating or rushing.

If used sparingly and authentically, encouragement is affirming.

Allow Silence and Don’t Interrupt

Silence in a conversation can be scary, but a pause allows the speaker to gather their thoughts and for you to digest what is being said.

Please don’t interrupt as this can appear disrespectful and distracting.

Reflect Back

Restate, but don’t repeat verbatim. Instead, paraphrase what you think the other party is saying with responses such as:

  • “What I’m hearing is…”
  • “Let me see if I’m following you….”

Reflecting on what you’ve heard signals you’re attempting to understand fully.

Clarify

  • Ask relevant questions to make sure you understand.
  • Make them open-ended questions, if possible. A “yes” or “no” may confirm, but an explanation offers more information.
  • Probe for feelings.

Sometimes the emotions behind the words are more important than the words themselves when someone seeks to be understood.

Keep an Open-Mind

Defer judgments, whether agreement or disagreement, and don’t make assumptions.

Wait until the speaker is finished before formulating opinions.

It’s hard not to think about what you’ll say next, especially if there’s disagreement, but you miss what is being said if you’re thinking about your response.

Respond Appropriately

  • Be open and honest in your responses.
  • Share your thoughts, insights, and feelings, respectfully, and respectfully.
  • You can acknowledge the speaker’s concerns and ideas even if you disagree…especially when you disagree.
